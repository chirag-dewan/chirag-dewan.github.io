PARALLAX

Catching people who quietly steal AI models — by watching how they behave, never reading what they ask.

The problem

If you run an AI platform, here’s a nightmare that trips no alarm: someone quietly copies your model by hammering its API with thousands of clever little questions and learning from the answers. It’s called model extraction, and the maddening part is that every single request looks completely normal. The theft isn’t in any one question — it’s in the pattern of all of them.

The idea

What if you could spot the abuse without ever reading what people actually asked? Just watch the shape of their behaviour — how fast they ask, how long the answers are, when they show up, which models they target. Private by design: you never need to see the prompts. The motto I kept coming back to: see the pattern, not the content.

What I built

Fourteen behavioural detectors — request speed, token ratios, timing regularity, session structure, and so on. On synthetic data it looked flawless: a clean line between normal users and attackers. I felt great about it. That feeling did not last.

The gut-check

I pointed it at 16.9 million real authentication events from Los Alamos — 500 users, 87 of them actually compromised. The score: 0.45 AUC — worse than flipping a coin. Seven of my fourteen detectors were pointing the wrong way entirely: flagging the legitimate power users and waving the attackers through. Beautifully engineered, completely backwards.

The fix

So I did the unglamorous thing: worked out which signals were just noise, switched them off, and leaned hard on the one that actually held up — “this user’s behaviour just changed.” That got it to 0.68 AUC on the real data.

The lesson that fell out: detectors that memorise specific attack shapes don’t survive contact with a new domain — but detecting change from a baseline travels everywhere. Subtraction beat addition.

Where it still breaks

Then I tried to slip past it, and found three honest holes: normalise one signal and you duck under the line; blend a little attack traffic into a lot of normal traffic and you vanish into the crowd; go slow and patient and you never trip the threshold. I published all three, because pretending a detector is bulletproof is how it gets you burned.

Why it matters

Before anyone can weaponise a frontier model, they need access to it — and access leaves a behavioural trail. PARALLAX is the tripwire at the front gate: 0.68 AUC on real data, open source, and honest about exactly where it fails.
